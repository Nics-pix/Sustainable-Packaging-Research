Banana PseudoStem (Musa Acuminata) Bags and Supermarket Paper Bags as Packaging Alternatives

A research study on sustainable packaging solutions.

Introduction

Plastic pollution has pushed the search for eco-conscious packaging beyond paper bags, which still cause deforestation and emissions. Banana pseudostems, often discarded as waste, offer strong, biodegradable fibers that can be turned into bags. This study compares banana stem bags with supermarket paper bags to test their sustainability, durability, and potential as greener alternatives.

Statement of the Problem

This study aims to compare the effectiveness of banana stem bags and supermarket paper bags as eco-conscious packaging alternatives. Specifically, it seeks to answer the following:

1. What is the best formulation of the banana stem paper bag?

  • Paper bag 1: Banana pseudostem - 45%; Sodium hydroxide - 30%; Water - 25%
  • Paper bag 2: Banana pseudostem - 55%; Sodium hydroxide - 20%; Water - 25%
  • Paper bag 3: Banana pseudostem - 40%; Sodium hydroxide - 35%; Water - 25%

2. What are the characteristics of banana stem bags and supermarket paper bags in terms of:

  • a. Durability
  • b. Water resistance
  • c. Strength

Methodology

Research Design

This study used an experimental-comparative design to develop banana pseudostem bags and test them against supermarket paper bags. The bags were compared in terms of strength, durability, water resistance, and biodegradability. This approach ensured reliable results while addressing the need for sustainable packaging solutions.

Research Subject

This study uses banana pseudostems as the main raw material in making paper bags. Their rich fiber content makes them biodegradable, sustainable, and affordable. The process transforms agricultural waste into eco-friendly packaging solutions.

Research Instrument

  • Blender – used to pulp the softened banana fibers into finer consistency.
  • Calibrated weights or sand with digital scale – to measure carrying capacity of the bags.
  • Camera – to document production, testing, and biodegradability changes.
  • Cooking pot with heat source – to boil banana stems with lye solution to soften fibers.
  • Digital scale – for weighing materials consistently.
  • Drying tray/surface – to air-dry paper sheets.
  • Hanging test rig/stand with hook – for load-bearing tests.
  • Measuring cups and beakers – for accurate liquid/solid measurements.
  • Rolling pin – to flatten paper sheets.
  • Ruler or caliper – to measure thickness, tear length, and dimensions.
  • Sewing kit – to assemble paper sheets into bags.
  • Screen or mold – to shape pulp into sheets.
  • Spray bottle/dropper – for controlled water absorbency testing.
  • Spring or luggage scale – to measure force before bag failure.
  • Stainless knife – for chopping banana stems.
  • Stopwatch – to record time for tests.
  • Strainer/cheesecloth – to filter excess liquid.
  • Thermometer – to monitor boiling temperature.
  • Water bath or tray – for water-resistance testing.

Data Gathering

  1. Seeking Approval: Researchers secure permission from adviser and school administration.
  2. Collection & Preparation: Banana pseudostems gathered, cleaned, and cut; materials prepared.
  3. Processing Fibers: Boiled in lye, rinsed, blended into pulp, strained to control thickness.
  4. Paper Bag Formation: Pulp spread on molds, pressed, sun-dried, trimmed, assembled with reinforced handles.
  5. Product Testing: Bags tested and compared with supermarket paper bags for quality and durability.

Research Instrument

Various instruments were used to ensure accurate and reliable testing, including drop setups for durability, soaking containers with balances for water resistance, and recording sheets with statistical software for data analysis. These tools ensured scientific standards were met, providing a strong basis for evaluating the potential of banana stem bags as alternatives to supermarket paper bags.

The study upheld strict ethical standards by seeking approval from authorities, recording data honestly, and ensuring fairness by testing both banana pseudostem and paper bags under the same conditions. Environmental responsibility was observed by using agricultural byproducts, avoiding harmful chemicals, and managing waste properly. References were properly cited to avoid plagiarism, and safety protocols such as wearing gloves and masks were followed to protect the researchers.

Literature Review

Properties of Banana Stem Fibers as Packaging Material

Banana pseudostems, usually discarded after harvest, contain strong natural fibers suitable for sustainable packaging. Studies show these fibers have high tensile strength (430–780 MPa), comparable to synthetic fibers, and contain 63–64% cellulose, which enhances both strength and biodegradability. Research also highlights their resistance to moisture, with low water absorption rates (7–9%) compared to paper, and faster decomposition (four weeks vs. six to eight for paper). Experiments with banana stem bags show they can carry up to 14 kg, outperforming paper bags in strength, flexibility, and usability. Overall, banana stem fibers offer environmental benefits and strong potential as eco-friendly packaging material.

Performance of Paper Bags as Packaging Alternative

Paper bags are widely used as plastic alternatives, but they have notable weaknesses. While they can carry 10–15 kg of dry goods, their strength decreases by 40% when wet, and they often fail with moist or irregularly shaped items. They degrade in six to eight weeks, faster than plastic but slower than fiber-based materials like banana or jute. Despite their popularity for being seen as eco-friendly, their production requires high energy and water, and their limited reusability reduces sustainability benefits. Overall, paper bags help reduce plastic use but are less durable and less environmentally efficient compared to agricultural fiber-based alternatives such as banana stem bags.

Innovative Approaches to Eco-Conscious Packaging

Research into eco-conscious packaging highlights the potential of agricultural byproducts such as jute-banana fiber, sugarcane bagasse, and pineapple leaf fiber. These materials show stronger durability, faster biodegradation, and better moisture resistance compared to conventional paper bags. They also help reduce landfill waste and cut greenhouse gas emissions by up to 30%. Tests on banana stem packaging further demonstrated superior strength and biodegradability, with complete decomposition in a few weeks. Overall, agricultural waste-based packaging offers both environmental and functional advantages, making banana stem bags a strong alternative to paper bags.
